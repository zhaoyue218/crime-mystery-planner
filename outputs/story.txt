The heavy, wet snow of a freak October blizzard hammered against the leaded glass of Blackwood Manor, sealing the gates and turning the surrounding English countryside into a monochromatic void. Inside, the air was thick with the scent of floor wax, expensive tobacco, and the sudden, chilling onset of mortality.

Detective Lena Marlowe stood in the Map Room, her coat still damp from the journey. At her feet lay Professor Adrian Wren, the country’s most formidable criminologist. He was sprawled across a mahogany table, his hand clutching an antique compass. To the uninitiated, it looked like a tragic heart failure.

"The house is sealed," Marlowe announced, her voice cutting through the hushed murmurs of the four guests gathered at the door. "The roads are flooded beneath the snow. No one leaves until I know why the Professor’s heart stopped so conveniently."

She knelt beside the body. While the others saw a natural collapse, Marlowe’s eyes found a tiny, jagged puncture wound at the base of Wren’s neck. Near his elbow, a display case for a medieval stiletto had been subtly shifted, its velvet lining disturbed as if a struggle had been whispered rather than shouted.

"He was going to tell us tonight," Evelyn Cross, their host, whispered. She was trembling, her silk gown rustling. "Adrian called us here for a 'reckoning.' He said he had discovered a rot at the heart of our circle. A fraud."

The suspicion shifted instantly to Nora Bell, the quiet archivist. Marlowe moved the investigation to the lecture antechamber, where a tea service sat abandoned. A cracked teacup caught the light; Nora’s fingerprints were stark against the porcelain.

"I served him," Nora admitted, her face pale. "But I went straight to the archive afterward to finish the cataloging. I have the logs to prove I was there at nine."

Marlowe followed Nora to the archive. The logbook indeed showed Nora’s signature at 9:00 PM, but as Marlowe scrutinized the ink, she noted a gap. Nora had checked out at 9:15 PM, leaving twenty minutes of the "murder window" wide open.

"A partial alibi is often worse than none at all, Miss Bell," Marlowe remarked.

In the observatory, she found Miles Dorian, the celebrated true-crime author, surrounded by wax cylinders. He was a man of practiced arrogance, but his hands shook as he lit a cigarette.

"Wren was a dinosaur," Dorian spat. "He threatened to ruin me. Claimed my latest book on the Sussex Slayings was plagiarized from his private notes. The man was a menace to my career."

Marlowe gestured to the Dictaphone on the desk. "You were here all evening?"

"Recording my latest chapter," Dorian claimed.

Marlowe played the wax cylinder. The author’s voice droned on, but the timeline was hollow. The recording ended abruptly at 9:10 PM, leaving a silence that lasted until Marlowe’s arrival. Like Nora, Miles Dorian had lost his shield of time.

Returning to the West Salon, Marlowe’s sharp eyes caught a flicker of movement in the fireplace. She reached into the dying embers with a poker, retrieving a charred scrap of a ledger. The words *'Embezzled—E. Cross'* were barely legible.

Evelyn Cross gasped, her hand flying to her throat. "That... that is an unrelated matter. Adrian found out I’d been ‘adjusting’ the estate’s charities. But I didn't kill him! I’m a thief, Detective, not a butcher."

Marlowe didn't answer. She was looking at the toxicology report she’d improvised using the Professor’s own field kit. The tea in the cracked cup was harmless. The poison wasn't ingested; it was digitalis, concentrated and delivered via the puncture wound Marlowe had spotted earlier. It was a method designed to mimic a heart attack—sophisticated, cold, and precise.

"It wasn't the tea," Marlowe murmured. "It was the map room."

She moved to the estate’s security office—a modern addition featuring an experimental "Electric Watchman" system that recorded the movement of doors and the state of the corridors. Julian Pike, the Professor's protégé, was already there, hovering near the equipment.

"I was checking the circuits," Pike said quickly. "The storm has been playing havoc with the lights."

Marlowe examined the paper ticker-tape of the Electric Watchman. "There’s a manual deletion here, Julian. The record for the east corridor—the path to the map room—was scrubbed at 9:20 PM. Only someone who understood this machinery could have bypassed it."

The tension in the house spiked. Marlowe headed back toward the Archive Room to re-question Nora, but found the room in shambles. Nora sat on the floor, breathless. 

"Someone was here," Nora cried. "A note Adrian left for me... it’s gone. It mentioned a cold case. The 'Derby Fabrication.' He said the data had been faked."

The final pieces began to click into place. Marlowe retreated to the guest wing, where she found the estate’s internal telephone logs. A message had been sent from Julian Pike’s room to the Map Room at 9:18 PM, luring the Professor to his death just before the lights failed.

"There was a generator fault," Marlowe noted, tracing wet, muddy boot prints from the basement corridor toward the east stairs. "A staged outage to mask the movement of a killer."

She gathered everyone in the Map Room one last time. The shadows of the clocktower loomed through the window, the snow finally beginning to taper off.

"Mistress Cross is a fraud," Marlowe began, her voice steady. "Mr. Dorian is a plagiarist. And Miss Bell was a convenient scapegoat. But none of you killed Adrian Wren."

She turned her gaze to Julian Pike. He stood near the disturbed display case, his expression a mask of helpful concern.

"The Professor wasn't just investigating a 'rot,'" Marlowe said. "He had found the ultimate sin in our profession. He discovered that you, Julian, fabricated the data in the famous Derby cold case study—the very work that made your career. He lured you here to demand a confession."

"That's absurd," Pike laughed, though his eyes remained cold. "I was in the basement fixing the generator."

"The generator you disabled," Marlowe countered. "The wet prints from the basement lead directly to the east stair, timed perfectly with the deletion on the Electric Watchman’s tape. You sent the internal page to Adrian, waited in the dark with the stiletto you’d coated in digitalis, and struck him when he reached for the compass. You thought the storm and the scandals of the others would bury the truth."

She stepped closer, holding up the internal phone extract and the security log. 

"You are the only one with the motive to protect a career built on lies, the technical knowledge to mask your movements, and the cold-blooded precision to use Adrian’s own criminological theories against him."

The silence in the room was absolute, broken only by the ticking of the grandfather clock. Julian Pike looked at the evidence in Marlowe’s hand, the weight of the logic pressing down on him. The arrogance drained from his face, replaced by a weary, jagged defeat.

"He was going to take everything," Pike whispered, his voice cracking. "I spent ten years building that reputation. He was going to destroy it in one night over a few 'adjusted' figures."

As the first gray light of dawn broke over the snow-covered manor, Julian Pike was led away to the study to wait for the authorities. Detective Marlowe stood by the window, watching the white landscape. The smallest contradiction had stopped looking small, and beneath the clocktower snow, the truth had finally been unearthed.
